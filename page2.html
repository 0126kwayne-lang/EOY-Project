Reptiles are air-breathing, ectothermic (cold-blooded) vertebrates covered in scales or scaly skin, encompassing over 12,000 species. Found worldwide in various habitats, major groups include snakes, lizards, turtles, and crocodilians. The largest living reptile is the saltwater crocodile, while some dwarf geckos are among the smallest.Global Reach: Reptiles are found on every continent except Antarctica. Because they are ectothermic, they rely on external heat (like the sun) to regulate their body temperature, making the frozen south the only place they can't survive. The Size Gap: The difference between the largest and smallest is staggering. A Saltwater Crocodile can grow to over 20 feet (6 meters), while the Nano-Chameleon (Brookesia nana) from Madagascar is small enough to sit on a fingernail. Ancient History: Many of the groups you mentioned, especially turtles and crocodilians, have remained relatively unchanged for over 200 million years, meaning they were thriving long before the first T. rex appeared. The "Third Eye": Some reptiles, like the Tuatara from New Zealand, actually have a "parietal eye" on the top of their heads. It has a lens and retina and helps them sense light cycles and seasons.

"Butt Breathing": Some species of turtles can actually extract oxygen from the water through their cloaca (essentially their rear end) to stay underwater for long periods. No Teeth: Unlike most other reptiles, turtles do not have teeth; they have sharp, keratin beaks similar to a bird's. Species to Explore: Leatherback Sea Turtle: The largest turtle in the world, it has a leathery shell instead of a hard one and can weigh up to 2,000 pounds. Alligator Snapping Turtle: Known for its prehistoric look and a tongue that looks like a worm to lure in fish. Snakes: The Sleek Predators Snakes have evolved incredible ways to survive without any limbs, and they are much more misunderstood than they are scary. "Stereo" Smelling: Snakes use their forked tongues to pick up chemical cues from the air, which they "taste" using an organ on the roof of their mouth to tell which direction a smell is coming from. Heat Vision: Some snakes, like pythons and pit vipers, have special "pits" on their faces that let them see the heat coming off warm-blooded animals, even in total darkness. No Eyelids: Snakes never blink because they don't have eyelids; instead, a clear scale called a "brille" protects their eyes. Species to Explore Hognose Snake: Popular on YouTube for their "dramatic" behavior—they will roll over and play dead if they feel threatened. Emerald Tree Boa: Famous for its vibrant green color and the way it coils perfectly over tree branches.

Reptiles live in diverse, mainly warm environments worldwide (excluding Antarctica), ranging from terrestrial deserts to aquatic marine habitats. They are ectothermic, requiring habitats with specific temperature gradients (20–30°C) for basking and cooling, alongside proper humidity, UVB lighting, and, for pets, appropriate substrate.
