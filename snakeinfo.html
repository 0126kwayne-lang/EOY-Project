Snakes are elongated, legless carnivorous reptiles (suborder Serpentes) found worldwide except near the poles, with over 3,700 species. As ectothermic (cold-blooded) amniotes, they regulate body temperature externally and shed their skin, known as ecdysis. While roughly 600 species are venomous, only about 200 can significantly wound or kill humans. National Geographic National Geographic +5 Key Snake Information Diet: All snakes are carnivores, eating rodents, insects, birds, eggs, and other reptiles. They possess highly kinetic skulls with detached jaws, allowing them to consume prey much larger than their heads. Venom & Hunting: Venomous snakes use specialized fangs to inject toxins, often categorized as neurotoxins or hemotoxins. Non-venomous snakes typically use constriction to kill prey. Senses: Snakes lack external ears and eyelids, relying on a transparent eye scale. They "smell in stereo" using a forked tongue to collect scent particles and the Jacobson's organ to detect them. Habitat & Locomotion: They inhabit various environments, including trees, underground tunnels, and sea snakes live in the ocean. Their four main types of locomotion include lateral undulation, concertina, rectilinear, and sidewinding. Reproduction: Snakes are primarily oviparous (egg-laying), but some are ovoviviparous (giving birth to live young). Terminology: A group of snakes is often called a bed, knot, pit, or nest. Lifespan: Many small species live around 4–15 years, while larger species can live 30+ years.
